Our group's research centers around the concept of modularity in systems biology. Here are some relevant papers, organised by theme.

Modularity Theory
01 The Architecture of Complexity Joe
Herbert A. Simon  ·  Facets Of Systems Science (1991)
+
Key thoughts (quoted from paper): 1. the frequency with which complexity takes the form of hierarchy-the complex system being composed of subsystems that, in turn, have their olvn subsystems, and so on. 2. theorizes about the relation between the structure of a complex system and the time required for it to emerge through evolutionary processes : specifically, it argues that hierarchic systems will evolve far more quickly than non-hierarchic systems of comparable size. 3. explores the dynamic properties of hierarchically organized systems, and shows how they can be decomposed into subsystems in order to analyze their behavior. 4. examines the relation between coillplex systems and their descriptions.

Boolean Networks & Control
04 The Dynamics of Conjunctive and Disjunctive Boolean Network Models Sai
Abdul Salam Jarrah, Reinhard Laubenbacher, Alan Veliz-Cuba  ·  Bulletin Of Mathematical Biology (2010)
+
A beautiful paper showing how the modular structure of a conjunctive network determines its number of steady states. It extends an earlier result giving a complete attractor description for Boolean networks with a single module. Interestingly, if NOT gates are introduced, the number of steady states collapses to 1 regardless of the module topology or poset structure.
DOI: 10.1007/s11538-010-9501-z ↗

Applied Category Theory
10 Structured versus Decorated Cospans Adittya
John C. Baez, Kenny Courser  ·  Compositionality (2022)
+
If we assume modularity implies compositionality, i.e. a way to study the behaviour of a system by studying the behaviour of its subsystems and the way the subsystems are interconnected, then this paper develops general mathematical frameworks based on monoidal double category theory, namely structured cospans and decorated cospans, to study systems' behaviour from the principle of compositionality. The authors demonstrated their general theory in concrete systems such as graphs, electrical circuits, Petri nets, reaction networks and dynamical systems. From a biological perspective, this compositional viewpoint may offer a principled way to reason about when experimentally identified subsystems such as pathways or regulatory modules can be treated as approximately independent, and when interconnections between them fundamentally alter their dynamical behavior.
DOI: 10.32408/compositionality-4-3 ↗
11 Algebras of open dynamical systems on the operad of wiring diagrams Adittya
Dmitry Vagner, David I. Spivak, Eugene Lerman  ·  Theory and Applications of Categories (2015)
+
Assuming modularity implies compositionality, this paper develops a general mathematical framework based on the theory of operads and operad algebras to study dynamical systems by interconnecting the dynamics of the subsystems. Quoting the relevant portion from the abstract: "In this paper, we use the language of operads to study open dynamical systems. More specifically, we study the algebraic nature of assembling complex dynamical systems from an interconnection of simpler ones. The syntactic architecture of such interconnections is encoded using the visual language of wiring diagrams. We define the symmetric monoidal category W, from which we may construct an operad OW, whose objects are black boxes with input and output ports, and whose morphisms are wiring diagrams, thus prescribing the algebraic rules for interconnection. We then define two W-algebras G and L, which associate semantic content to the structures in W. Respectively, they correspond to general and to linear systems of differential equations, in which an internal state is controlled by inputs and produces outputs."
DOI: 10.70930/tac/7tz1nkbd ↗
12 Obstructions to Compositionality Adittya
Nihil Shah, Paolo Perrone  ·  EPTCS (2023)
+
If we assume modularity implies compositionality, then it follows that non-compositionality implies non-modularity. Quoting the relevant portion from the abstract: "Formally, we introduce invariants of categories that we call zeroth and first homotopy posets, generalising in a precise sense the π₀ and π₁ of a groupoid. These posets can be used to obtain a qualitative description of how far an object is from being terminal and a morphism is from being iso. In the context of applied category theory, this formal machinery gives us a way to qualitatively describe the 'failures of compositionality', seen as failures of certain (op)lax functors to be strong, by classifying obstructions to the (op)laxators being isomorphisms." This approach may suggest a general mathematical framework for studying evolved biological systems that exhibit partial or context-dependent modularity.
DOI: 10.4204/EPTCS.397.14 ↗
